## Stellar lifetime
The time it takes for a star to **radiate all the star's energy** is calculated by:
$$
t=\dfrac{E}{L}
$$
where $E$ is the energy, and $L$ is the [[3_Luminosity#Luminosity|luminosity]] (rate that star emits energy).

Energy $E$ comes from [[1_Star Formation#Nuclear fusion|nuclear fusion]] using Einstein's mass-energy equivalence. Since the mass of the $\text{H}$ available for fusion is **proportional** to the mass of the **entire star**, $E$ is related to the mass of the star.

$$
\begin{align}
E=mc^{2} &  & m \propto M_{\text{star}}
\end{align}
$$
$$
\implies E\propto M \implies { t \propto \dfrac{M}{L} }
$$

For a [[2_Main Sequence#Main Sequence|main sequence]] star, the [[2_Main Sequence#Determining mass|mass-luminosity relation]] holds:
$$
L \propto M^{4}
$$
$$
\implies t\propto\dfrac{M}{M^{4}} \implies \boxed{ t\propto \dfrac{1}{M^{3}} }
$$

This means that **more massive** stars have **shorter lifetime**. Although they have more fuel to burn, the rate that they burn fuel is much faster.

### Red Giants
After all the $\text{H}$ in the star's core has turned into $\text{He}$, there is **no more pressure** to push outwards.

**Gravity** overcomes, making the $\text{He}$ core contract and heat up. This is hot enough to fuse the $\text{H}$ in the layer *just* outside the core into $\text{He}$ as well.

The additional energy is **more than enough** to balance gravity, and the **star expands**. This leads to the surface layers cooling down, turning red.

The stars in this stage are called **Red Giants**. The increase in luminosity and decrease in temperature makes the star go (↑ →) in the [[2_Main Sequence#The HR Diagram|HR diagram]].

![[chart42A.png|400]]

During the Red Giant phase, the star loses around $10-20 \text{ \%}$ of mass.

### Planetary Nebulae
Eventually the **outer layers** are **blown away**. The $\text{He}$ core contracts due to gravity, and it becomes hot enough to fuse $\text{C}$ (and some $\text{O}$).

When all of the $\text{He}$ becomes $\text{C}$, the core collapses and generates heat, becoming a Red Giant **a second time**. This is a short and convulsive phase.

> [!tip] Planetary nebulae have nothing to do with planets. It is about the size of a planet from Earth, but it is very large.

The outer layers that are blown away **form two lobes**. The apparent shape depends on the viewing angles.

### White dwarfs
As the $\text{C}$ core collapses under gravity, it reaches the limit of **electron degeneracy pressure**, the densest state of a regular matter.

![[Screenshot 2026-03-18 at 2.42.29 PM.png|400]]<span style="display:inline-block;width:100%;text-align:center;font-style:italic;">people.umass.edu</span>

> [!tip] The maximum mass of the core for Electron Degeneracy pressure to hold up gravity is $1.4 \text{ }M_{\odot}$

White dwarfs are **very hot** ($10,000\text{ K}+$), and shines the most in the UV. They are **dim** (low luminosity). They are about the **size of Earth**, due to the compression of the core.

![[chart43A.png|400]]

The path of the white dwarfs as shown in the [[2_Main Sequence#Gaia's HR Diagram|Gaia's HR diagram]] shows that the $\text{C}$ core crystallizes into **giant diamonds** as they cool down.

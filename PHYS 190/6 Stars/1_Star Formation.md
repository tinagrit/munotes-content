## Self-gravity
To form a star, we need **gravity** to **pull gas together**.
- However, gas pressure prevents that action
- We need **gravity to win**

> [!tip] Gas needs to be **cold and dense** to form a star. [[4_Molecular Hydrogen#Molecular Clouds|Molecular Clouds]] is an ideal site.

### Pressure and temperature
- [[1_Lights and Colours#Temperature|Temperature]] refers to **how fast** particles move
- Pressure refers to **how hard** particles **hit each other**

The higher the temperature, the higher the pressure
$$
\text{pressure} \propto \text{temperature}
$$

This means that if we want low pressure (so gravity wins), we want **low temperature**.

## What powers stars

Historical:
- 4th century BCE, Aristotle: stars are stones flying through the sky and catching fire
- 1854,1861, Helmholtz, Kelvin: gravitational contraction causes heating, which could power the Sun for 18 million years
- 1919, Eddington: variable stars show that contraction theory is false
- 1921, Holmes: evidence that Earth is a few billion years old, way longer than 18 million years
- **1920-1930, Aston, Gamow, Bethe**: converting $^{1}\text{H}$ into $^{4}\text{He}$ could provide enough energy to power stars

### Nuclear fusion
It takes 4 of $^{1}\text{H}$ nuclei to create 1 $^{4}\text{He}$ nucleus.

While forming, there is a **difference in mass**:
- 4 of $\text{H}$ nuclei weigh $6.693\times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}$
- 1 $\text{He}$ nucleus weighs $6.645 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}$
- There is $0.048 \times 10^{-27}\text{ kg}$ of **extra mass** (*mass defect*)

According to Einstein's mass–energy equivalence,
$$
E=mc^{2}
$$
where $E$ is energy, $m$ is mass, and $c$ is the [[2_Scientific Notation#Speed of light|speed of light]].

The mass defect is converted into **energy** that powers stars.

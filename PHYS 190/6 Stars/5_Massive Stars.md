## Continued fusing
A star larger than $8\text{ }M_{\odot}$ is **very rare**. Unlike [[4_Stellar Lifetime#White dwarfs|smaller stars]], heavier elements are fused.

For example, for a $25\text{ }M_{\odot}$ star,
- fuses $\text{H}\to \text{He}$ takes 7,000,000 years
- fuses $\text{He}\to \text{C}$ takes 700,000 years
- fuses $\text{C}\to \text{O}$ takes 600 years
- fuses $\text{O}\to \text{Si}$ takes 6 months
- fuses $\text{Si}\to \text{Fe}$ takes 1 day
- Core collapsing takes a quarter of a second

The core collapses since its mass is [[4_Stellar Lifetime#White dwarfs|greater than]] $1.4 \text{ }M_{\odot}$, making gravity overcome electron degeneracy. As this happens, the **electrons and protons combine**.
$$
p^{+}+e^{-}\to \underbrace{ n }_{ \text{neutrons} }+\underbrace{ v_{e} }_{ \text{nutrinos} }
$$
Similar to electron degeneracy pressure, this creates **Neutron degeneracy pressure**. The maximum mass that this can hold up gravity is $3 \text{ }M_{\odot}$.


### Supernova Explosion
As the core collapses, the outer layers come **crashing down** on the neutron core at ~$25,000\text{ km/s}$. The bounce transfers momentum to the outer layers, causing a supernova explosion.

![[Screenshot 2026-03-18 at 3.24.26 PM.png|300]]<span style="display:inline-block;width:100%;text-align:center;font-style:italic;">SN 1054 remnants</span>

Supernova explosion creates **neutron stars**. A nucleus is about $10^{5}$ times smaller than the entire atom, this makes neutron stars **very small**, about the size of a city, but also **very dense**.

Neutron stars are **pulsars**. 
- They emit a **beam** of radiation and particles as they spin around their axis
- Period of pulsars range from $1/1000\text{ s}$ to $10\text{ s}$. 
- As the beam shines over Earth, we can see them "pulse"
- Pulsating **radio sources** can be detected from Earth

## Black hole
A star that is larger than $40\text{ }M_{\odot}$ is **extremely rare**. Its core will be larger than $3\text{ }M_{\odot}$, and cannot sustain as a neutron star (gravity overcomes neutron degeneracy pressure).

It collapses **into a black hole**.

### Escape velocity
In a black hole, the escape velocity is **greater than the speed of light**.

The escape velocity of Earth is $11.2\text{ m/s}$, which is the speed that an object can escape the orbit.
$$
v_{\text{esc}}^{2}=\dfrac{2GM}{R_{\text{S}}}
$$
where $R_{\text{S}}$ is the **Schwarzschild radius**.

If a black hole has the mass of the Sun, the $R_{\text{S}}\approx 3\text{ km}$, meaning light within that radius cannot leave the black hole.


### Evidence of black hole
1. There is evidence of a star close to the black hole, and the material of the star getting sucked in. In the case of **Cygnus X-1 binary stars**, the speed that they spiral inward (accretion disk) are so fast that it causes a lot of friction, which causes it to heat up, and the heat so hot () would shine in the X-rays.

2. Evident **gravitational waves**. Detected as two black holes combine.

3. Direct imaging by the *Event Horizon Telescope* in the **galaxy M87** and **Milky Way**

4. The orbit of stars in the **Galactic Centre** finds the black hole of mass $4\text{ million }M_{\odot}$


### Supermassive Black Hole
SMBHs grow **very quickly**, they are observed when the universe was < 1 billion years old.

The formation of SMBH is unknown, but theories include:
- **Dense cluster** of stars collapsed into a black hole
- **Massive cloud** collapsed directly into a black hole, skipping the stars phase

The "seed" black holes are from $10-100 \text{ M}_{\odot}$, and grow by devouring stars and other black holes.

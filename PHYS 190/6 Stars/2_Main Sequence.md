## The HR Diagram
*Hertzsprung* (1911) and *Russel* (1913) discovered independently that **luminosity and temperature** of stars are related.

Stars are plotted where the:
- x-axis is the [[5_Stellar Absorption#Spectral types|Spectral classes]], colour index, or temperature, ← means hotter
- y-axis is the [[3_Luminosity#Luminosity|luminosity]], ↑ means brighter

## Main Sequence
When a star is formed, hydrogens are collided to create **helium** in a process called **nuclear fusion**.

Stars that begin nuclear fusion in their cores **populate** the **Main Sequence** (*MS*).

![[Screenshot 2026-03-06 at 1.13.27 PM.png|450]]<span style="display:inline-block;width:100%;text-align:center;font-style:italic;">OpenStax Astronomy Fig. 18.15</span>


> [!tip] 90% of all stars are in the Main Sequence, including the Sun
> Stars spend 90% of their lifetime burning $\text{H}$ into $\text{He}$, and therefore to our "universe snapshot", 90% of stars are Main Sequence stars.


### Spectroscopic Parallax
If we see an **unknown star** that we want to find the distance of, there is a **90% chance** that it is a Main Sequence star.

Measure its colour in $\text{B}-\text{V}$ or spectral type, and use the HR diagram to get its **luminosity**.

Compare the luminosity with its [[2_Stellar Brightness#Apparent magnitude|apparent brightness]], and calculate for distance.


### Determining size
Recall the [[3_Blackbody Radiation#Stefan-Boltzmann law|Stefan-Boltzmann law]]:
$$
I\propto T^{4}
$$
where $I$ is the **intensity** (watts per square metre) and $T$ is **temperature** in $\text{K}$.

By the definition of $I$, we have that
$$
I=\dfrac{L}{A}=\dfrac{L}{4\pi R^{2}}
$$
where $L$ is the **luminosity** and $A$ is the **area** (use $4\pi R^{2}$ to assume a sphere)

It follows that:
$$
\boxed{ \dfrac{L}{R^{2}}\propto T^{4} }
$$


> For example, for stars that have the same luminosity, which ones are larger?
> ![[Screenshot 2026-03-08 at 1.11.11 PM.png|400]]

Since luminosity is constant, we have that:
$$
\dfrac{L}{R^{2}}\propto T^{4} \implies \dfrac{1}{R^{2}}\propto T^{4} \implies \dfrac{1}{R} \propto T^{2}
$$

This means that:
- **higher** temperature would result in **smaller** radius (← smaller)
- **lower** temperature would result in **larger** radius (→ larger)

This is how we label the group of stars to the right of the main sequence the **Red Giants**

### Determining mass

![[chart39A.png|500]]

Stars that are **massive** refers to them having **more mass**.

In the Main Sequence, stars to the **top left** (higher luminosity and higher temperature) are **more massive** than stars to the bottom right.
$$
L \propto M^{3.5} \approx \boxed{ L \propto M^{4} }
$$

> [!warning] This  is only true for stars in the **Main Sequence** (aka. stars that are in the process of nuclear fusion)

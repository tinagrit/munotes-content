## Model History

**The Herschel Model**
- First known model of the galaxy
- *William* and *Caroline Herschel* observed stars
- They conclude that the Sun lives in the centre of a **flat stellar disk**.

**Harlow Shapley**
- Found that globular clusters distributed in a **sphere**
- The centre of the sphere is far from the Sun, about $15\text{ kpc}$

The Herschels couldn't see the rest of the disk **due to dust**.

### Thin disk
- about $120\text{ pc}$ thick
- consists of **younger stars**
- has **most** of the stellar mass ($4\times 10^{10} \text{ M}_{\odot}$)
- [[3_Ionized Hydrogen#Ionized Hydrogen (HII)|HII regions]] and [[4_Molecular Hydrogen#Molecular Clouds|molecular clouds]]

### Thick disk
- about $600\text{ pc}$ thick
- consists of **older stars**
- only has mass of a few percent of thin disk
- not much gas, dust

### Stellar halo
- about a sphere of $30\text{ kpc}$ around the Milky Way
- only consists of $2\%$ of the stars, these are **very old**
- has a lot of mass, but not as much as thin disk ($10^{10}\text{ M}_{\odot}$)
- very little gas, dust
- orbit is **randomly tilted**

### Bulge
- peanut shape in the middle of thin disk
- suggests that the Milky Way **has a bar** about $3\text{ kpc}$ long, due to the $\infty$ shaped orbit
- consists of stars slightly younger than thick disk
- has **half the mass** as the disk

### Galactic Centre
- where the [[5_Massive Stars#Black hole|black hole]] is
- difficult to see since the Milky Way has **a lot of dust**
	- needs to be seen through **radio waves** using the VLA (*Very Large Array* of radio telescopes)
- stars orbit a dark object of about $4\times 10^{6}\text{ M}_{\odot}$ within $120\text{ AU}$, an SMBH (**supermassive black hole**)

> [!tip] Our black hole has the [[5_Massive Stars#Escape velocity|Schwarzschild radius]] of about 0.12 AU.

Using the stars' orbit, precise measurement of our **distance** to the Galactic Centre came to be $8\text{ kpc}$.

## Spiral Arms

Many galaxies that have disks are observed to have **spiral arms** in the **thin disk**.

![[Screenshot 2026-03-20 at 4.43.42 PM.png|350]]<span style="display:inline-block;width:100%;text-align:center;font-style:italic;">ESA</span>

There are two ways to know if a galaxy has spiral arms
- **Distribution** **of** **blue stars**. Notice that the stars in the spiral pattern are blue stars ($\text{O}$ and $\text{B}$)
- **Velocity of** [[2_Neutral Hydrogen#Neutral Hydrogen (HI)|HI clouds]], detecting $21\text{ cm}$ from spin-flip transition, and seeing if it is [[4_Doppler Effect#Redshift and blueshift|redshift or blueshift]]

We've detected **two main arms** in the Milky Way, including
- *Perseus* arm
- *Scutum-Centaurus* arm
- other minor arms such as Orion arm


### Density-Wave Theory
Explains why only **young stars** are found in the spiral arms, and other stars evenly distributed around the disk.

Spiral arms are **not rigid structure**, but **regions of density**.
- Stars move right through the region with no issues
- Gas **slams** into the dense wave, leading to [[1_Star Formation#Self-gravity|star formation]]

![[Screenshot 2026-03-20 at 4.54.35 PM.png|500]]<span style="display:inline-block;width:100%;text-align:center;font-style:italic;">universe-review.ca</span>

The $\text{O}$ and $\text{B}$ stars **don't live long** enough to leave the arm.

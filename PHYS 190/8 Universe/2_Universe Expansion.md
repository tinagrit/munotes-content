## Hubble-Lemaitre Law

Hubble and Lemaitre showed the [[4_Doppler Effect#Redshift and blueshift|redshift]] of the most [[4_Galaxy Classification#Spiral galaxies|spiral nebulae]], concluding that they are moving **away from us** and that the **universe is expanding**.

Hubble mapped the **distance and velocity** of [[3_Distance to Galaxies#Type Ia Supernova|Type Ia supernovae]] into a ***Hubble diagram***. This plot shows that the farther an object is, the faster it is moving away from us.

![[Screenshot 2026-04-03 at 5.49.05 PM.png|400]]

The best fit line through the points is a linear function through origin,
$$
y=mx
$$
Substituting we have:
$$
\boxed{ v=H_{0}d }
$$
where $H_{0}$ is the **Hubble constant**, which is about $73\text{ km/s/Mpc}$.


### Uniform expansion
The Hubble-Lemaitre law is consistent with the idea that the **universe expands uniformly**.

Spaces **between** objects are increased at the **same scale**, and resulting in further objects moving away faster.

![[chart47A.png|600]]

While galaxies aren't moving themselves, we **detect redshift** since as space expands, wavelengths expand too.

## Age of the Universe
Using the standard velocity formula:
$$
v=\dfrac{d}{t} \implies t=\dfrac{d}{v}
$$
Substituting the Hubble-Lemaitre Law ($v=H_{0}d$), we have:
$$
t=\dfrac{\cancel{ d }}{H_{0}\cancel{ d }}=\dfrac{1}{H_{0}}
$$
This $t$ is called **Hubble time**.
The Hubble time is the **age of the universe**, which is when the Big Bang happened.

Calculating the Hubble time, we get around $13.8$ billion years.

### Measuring far distance
The further a galaxy is, the further **back in time** we see. Since the furthest galaxies move away from us quickly, we say that the galaxy **is at redshift X**.

### Rate of expansion
The future of the universe depends on the matter in it:
- **Too much** matter leads to too much gravity, and the universe will **collapse**
- **Too little** matter leads to too little gravity. and the universe will **expand forever**

Since further galaxies represent the [[2_Universe Expansion#Measuring far distance|universe in the past]], by measuring these galaxies, we find out that
- the Universe was expanding **slower in the past**
- the Universe is **accelerating** in its expansion
This does not fit any of the universe models that we made. 

This acceleration requires energy that we don't know of, **dark energy**. This energy pushes things apart.
*Not to be confused with* [[2_Dark Matter#Dark Matter|dark matter]].
